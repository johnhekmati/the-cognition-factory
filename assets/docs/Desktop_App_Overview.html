The desktop app — what you open and what it does

The website explains the idea. The desktop app is where the loop runs on your machine: deep learning, honest practice, and a clean place to stop when life interrupts — with a side chat co-pilot when you need one.

In one line. A local shell for map · practice · save place — not another browser tab that forgets you, and not a public app-store marketplace.

Who it is for

Busy adults and small households who want skill that sticks under real calendars. One install can support more than one learner (each with their own profile and materials). Institutions evaluating the product can treat this as a picture of the learner-facing surface; deeper deployment options live in the enterprise and LMS guides.

What it is (and is not)

It is

  • A Windows-first desktop app (household / personal install today).
  • A place to load your study materials, work a short block, and leave a handoff for tomorrow.
  • Wired for a capable AI chat model you configure (the app is the structure; the model helps you think under pressure).
  • Aligned with the same design language as thecognitionfactory.com.

It is not

  • A content library or binge course player.
  • A multi-tenant cloud LMS by itself.
  • A guarantee of outcomes — you still do the work.

The three main doors

These are the load-bearing rooms. Everything else supports them.

Deep learning

One idea until it clicks. You load a topic, work with structure and the side chat, and aim for a picture of the subject you can return to — not only a chat that will scroll away.

Practice

Quiz yourself the way the real world will: honest right/wrong pressure when it counts, then clear next work from what you missed — not random coverage of everything.

Save place

When you stop (or life stops you), leave a short note for next-time-you: where you were and what you meant to do next. Tomorrow starts from that board, not from memory.

The quieter tools

Useful shells around the three doors — not a second product to learn.

  • Home — lobby: who you are, what stretch of day it is, what’s active, one clear path into work.
  • Progress — goals, accuracy, and a sense of what to do next (not a vanity streak counter).
  • Notes — a simple study scratchpad while you work.
  • Profiles — who is studying on this machine (photo, bio, household access as needed).
  • Settings — chat readiness, layout prefs, and household-admin options when relevant.
  • Help — in-app guide and a path to contact support.

How a short session usually runs

  1. Open Home — see what you’re already in the middle of.
  2. Pick Deep learning or Practice.
  3. Load materials & chat when you’re ready to work that block.
  4. Talk with the side co-pilot where it helps (each main door keeps its own chat).
  5. When you stop: Save place → one line: Next time I will…
  6. Optional: glance Progress or jot something in Notes.
Clock labels. Labels like “this morning,” “today,” and “tonight” follow your computer’s clock. The app is talking to when you are, not a fixed “study night.”

Chat, in plain terms

  • Chat is separate by section — Practice does not wipe Deep learning, and vice versa.
  • The co-pilot shows up on rooms that need it; quieter screens stay quiet.
  • No API key configured? That’s a household-admin setup step — Help points you there.

Names, if you see them

Public site and app often say Deep learning · Practice · Save place. When product codes appear, they are just the engines inside The Cognition Factory:

  • HAL-E — Deep learning
  • AAE — Practice
  • CSS — Save place

You do not need those codes to use the app.

What we intentionally do not cover here

How materials are authored, how files are laid out on disk, or internal operator workflows. Those live with implementers and partners under separate briefs — not in the public learner path.
